Regulate Airbnb in Manhattan

Congressman Ben Kallos 
3 days ago

Should NYC gov legislate to control Airbnb activities?

A whole new way of renting houses and vacations spots arose when Airbnb came up. An inovative idea and an iconic case study of so-called sharing economy, this community is a marketplace where guests can book spaces from hosts connects people who have space to spare with those who are looking for a place to stay. It’s goal, they say: to make people feel home anywhere in the world.

But the reality is far less benign, according to a report released by Eric T. Schneiderman, the New York attorney general.

The report will say nearly three-quarters of all Airbnb rentals in the city are illegal, violating zoning or other laws such as the Multiple Dwelling Law. Commercial operators, not hard-luck residents, supply more than a third of the units and generate more than a third of the revenue. At least a handful of landlords are running what amount to illegal hostels. Property owners on Airbnb are indeed making money, but it is not being spread around. Most rentals are in three high-profile Manhattan neighborhoods. Queens, the Bronx and Staten Island barely figure.

Arguments Against:

Admirers of Airbnb say these stars of the so-called sharing economy are breaking up monopolies that have grown greedy and lazy. They are empowering individuals.In some contentious spots, like San Francisco, where the local government endorsed a plan last week to essentially legalize Airbnb, a resolution may be in sight. But in New York, where real estate is often viewed as a blood sport, the battle is only deepening.

Arguments in Favor:

Those in favour of making legislation to regulate Airbnb activities say that the start-ups are unsavory efforts to avoid regulation and taxes, and that the very term “sharing economy” is ridiculous.“The greatest problem is the threat to tenants by owners who hope to vacate as many units as possible, or even entire buildings, to then be used as transient, illegal hotels,” says Manhattan Borough President Gale Brewer “Anyone operating an illegal hotel should be on notice that the state and city will take aggressive enforcement actions in this area,” said Ney York Attorney General. “A slick advertising campaign doesn’t change the fact that this is illegal activity.”

What's next

We do not want large owners to avoid taxes under the pretext of sharing-economy, although it does seem good that people can pay their bills being hospitable. Airbnb is good for house owners that want to share costs recieving people at their homes, but it’s even better for landlords owning entire buildings that work as illegal hotels. At the end of the day, it is again about our tax system. If many aspects of Airbnb are good for cities, why should we legislate against instead of just controling mean people with flexible laws? Maybe the law is wrong, and we shoul be doing something to fix it. Airbnb-like startups should cooperate. Councilmembers and researchers and clients should cooperate. We’d probably come up with a solution that’s good enough for most people involved.

We want to know your reasons to vote yes or no. In this case, arguments are more important than votes
